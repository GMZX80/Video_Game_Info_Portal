PHASE 0

From Play to Pay

How screens became games, homes became studios, and code became a North East business.

Before there was a lab, there was a chain of inventions: displays people could play, computers they could afford, code they were invited to alter, and companies willing to turn experiments into products. This opening chapter follows that chain into Newcastle and Tyneside.

The opening argument

The first video game is the wrong question.

There is no single finish line called first. Electronic play, computer play, public play, raster video, the home console, the arcade machine and the commercial industry all begin at different moments.

Phase 0 argument: video game history is not merely a race to identify one first game. It is a sequence of thresholds: display, interaction, access, programmability, reproduction, distribution, ownership, and revenue.

“The history becomes clearer when we stop asking who arrived first and start asking what became possible next.”

01

Display

Was the image a computer CRT, an oscilloscope trace, a vector display or a raster-scan television?

02

Interaction

Could a player change the visible state in real time, and did the machine respond as part of play?

03

Access

Could it be played only in a laboratory, by visitors at an open day, in an arcade or in the home?

04

Programmability

Was the system a fixed circuit, a stored program, a teachable language or a machine open to modification?

05

Reproduction

Could the experience be copied cheaply, manufactured at scale and understood without an expert nearby?

06

Distribution

Did the game travel through patents, cabinets, cartridges, cassettes, printed listings, shops or friends?

07

Ownership

Was it an experiment, shared code, a patented apparatus, a licence, a product or a company?

08

Revenue

Where did money enter the system: a coin slot, a console sale, a cassette, a licence or a publishing contract?

Research lens · Morgan & Lee

The pioneer patent was not a prize for inventing fun.

Graham Morgan and Jeffrey Lee’s analysis redirects the controversy toward the actual legal and technical claim: a commercially useful apparatus for generating and manipulating player-controlled symbols on a standard television-type raster display. The decisive question was not simply whether an earlier game existed. It was whether that earlier system disclosed what the patent claimed.

Origins of digital play

Four screens. Four different “firsts”.

OXO, Tennis for Two and Spacewar! are not footnotes to be swept aside. They are genuine, important early interactive games. But none is identical to the low-cost, standard-television raster apparatus that Ralph Baer and his collaborators documented, patented and brought toward manufacture.

This plural origin story keeps OXO, Tennis for Two, Spacewar!, the Brown Box, Magnavox Odyssey, Computer Space, and Pong in view without forcing them to answer the same historical question.

The pivot

Ralph Baer made the television negotiable.

Baer’s crucial insight was not that a dot could move on a cathode-ray tube. That had already happened. It was that a separate, inexpensive control unit could turn the ordinary television into an active family entertainment device.

With William Harrison and William Rusch, he built prototypes, kept engineering records, pursued patents and demonstrated the system to television manufacturers. This combination of technical design and commercial method is why the Brown Box and Magnavox Odyssey sit at the centre of the industry’s legal origin story.

01

Existing infrastructure. Use the TV people already owned.

02

Interactive symbols. Generate, move and detect relationships between objects.

03

Reconfigurable play. One platform, multiple game arrangements.

04

Commercial proof. Patent, licence, manufacture and defend.

Why did OXO, Tennis for Two and Spacewar! not simply destroy the pioneer patents?

Because earlier play is not automatically the same prior art. The court and the patents focused on the mechanisms used to represent and manipulate game symbols on a raster-scan television—especially player-controlled symbols, hitting, coincidence and response between objects. Earlier systems used different display architectures, contexts and implementations.

This does not make them “not games”. It means they did not disclose every element of the later claimed apparatus. The distinction is technical, legal and commercial—not a denial of their historical importance.

A forty-second detour into 2022

In 1973, Computer Space already looked like the future.

The arcade machine visible in Soylent Green, whose story was set in 2022, is Computer Space: created by Nolan Bushnell and Ted Dabney, published by Nutting Associates, and commercially derived from the ideas demonstrated by Spacewar!. The cabinet itself is not Spacewar!. It is a neat cultural marker: the arcade cabinet had become an image of tomorrow before the industry had fully learned how to make arcade play universal.

SINCLAIR

Why Clive Sinclair matters

His defining feature was not colour.
It was access.

Clive Sinclair’s decisive contribution was to keep asking how much could be removed from the price without removing the idea of a computer. He turned technical compromise into social scale. The ZX machines were small, idiosyncratic and sometimes frustrating—but they placed programming in homes by the million.

That achievement was collective. Richard Altwasser shaped much of the Spectrum hardware; Rick Dickinson created its industrial design; Steve Vickers, John Grant and Nine Tiles developed the BASIC lineage and documentation. Sinclair’s leadership was the commercial insistence that all of this should reach a mass audience.

“Maximum new facilities for the minimum money.”

The machine as teacher

The Spectrum did not boot into a shop. It booted into a language.

Sinclair BASIC was resident in ROM and available immediately after switch-on, not sold as an optional development product. The supplied programming manual moved from PRINT and loops to colour, graphics, tape storage, memory and machine code.

The approachable and the low-level were not separate worlds. A BASIC program could reserve memory with CLEAR, place byte values with DATA and POKE, inspect memory with PEEK, store routines with SAVE CODE, and transfer control with USR. Loaders, assemblers and magazine listings made the route from BASIC to Z80 machine code visible.

768 bytes8 × 8 colour attributes

A lesson from CSC3224

There were no friendly graphics objects underneath.

On the 48K Spectrum, the display was memory: 6,912 bytes, made from 6,144 bitmap bytes and 768 colour-attribute bytes. Software had to understand how the ULA read pixel and attribute bytes, and colour belonged to 8 × 8 character cells. Those colour-attribute constraints shaped game aesthetics as surely as they shaped code.

This is a useful bridge to the later Game Technology Lab: game engineering repeatedly advances by abstracting hardware complexity—while still knowing when the abstraction leaks.

Code Travels on Paper

Before software was downloaded, it could be printed.

Through magazines, books, newsletters, clubs, libraries, cassette swapping and friends, players formed an early software-distribution network. Readers did not merely consume games: they retyped them, debugged them, modified them and sent new programs back.

BASIC was a door, not a ceiling

Machine code could arrive as numbers in a magazine.

The Spectrum contained no built-in assembler, but BASIC could load byte values into memory and call them with USR. Commercial assemblers, books, loaders and magazine listings provided the next step.

This hybrid practice mattered to games. BASIC was excellent for learning and prototyping; games often combined BASIC loaders with machine-code routines for time-critical drawing, movement and sound. The path from beginner to systems programmer was visible on one machine.

From play to pay

Businessification: when code becomes a company.

A game is not yet a business when it runs. It becomes a business when a repeatable chain connects the author to a paying player: program → working game → cassette master → duplication → packaging and artwork → magazine advertising and reviews → mail order or retail → ports and licences → support, salaries, deadlines, and company risk.

North East case study

Tynesoft: the moment the story becomes local.

BLAYDONNEWCASTLE

Founded in Blaydon in 1983, Tynesoft makes “businessification” visible. At Tynesoft, educational software became games and publishing. Individual programs became a catalogue. A regional team reached a national market across multiple incompatible 8-bit computers.

Its importance is not that it was the only North East company, or that every release was a classic. It is that the complete industrial problem can be seen at a human scale: finding talent, choosing platforms, securing licences, converting games, manufacturing media, attracting press, reaching shops and surviving a hardware transition.

1983

Start with education

The company began around educational software, then moved quickly into the expanding games and publishing market.

1984–85

Turn culture into product

Games connected to familiar television properties—including Auf Wiedersehen Pet and Super Gran—gave home-computer players recognisable worlds.

1986–89

Grow through breadth

Original titles, event-based sports games, licensed conversions and the MicroValue budget label spread risk across formats and price points.

1990

Meet the platform transition

The move from 8-bit to 16-bit development raised production expectations and cost. Tynesoft ceased trading, but people and expertise continued into successor companies.

The engineering lesson

“Multi-format” did not mean pressing Export.

Each platform could differ in processor, memory map, display modes, colour constraints, sound, storage and controller conventions. A port was often a new implementation under the pressure of a shared licence and release window.

The work included cassette duplication, artwork, retail, licences and reviews because the catalogue had to serve multiple incompatible 8-bit computers. Tynesoft’s support for less-served machines was commercially astute: it found audiences with fewer alternatives. The same choice multiplied testing, documentation and support. Platform breadth was both advantage and debt.

The regional lesson

The North East was connected to the national boom.

The classic “bedroom coder” image is useful but incomplete. Tynesoft shows offices, teams, local suppliers and a regional labour market. Games were not only made in an imagined southern centre; technical and commercial capability grew around the Tyne.

That matters to the later Newcastle University story. A lab concerned with industry did not need to invent a regional relationship to games from nothing. It could build on a history of making, publishing and adapting them.
