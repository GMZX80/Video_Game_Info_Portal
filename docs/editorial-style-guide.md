# Editorial Style Guide

Generated: 2026-06-18

## Voice

Write like a carefully researched magazine feature in a museum space:

- human, clear and direct;
- character-led where evidence allows;
- careful about uncertainty;
- readable before it is comprehensive;
- specific without pretending omniscience.

Prefer short paragraphs, strong section openings and captions that explain why an object matters.

## Evidence Language

Use evidence-forward formulations:

- `X recalls...`
- `Contemporary credits identify...`
- `Company records show...`
- `A later interview describes...`
- `The evidence does not yet establish...`
- `This record supports publication, not development...`
- `This is a staff-heritage lead, not legal continuity.`

Avoid:

- invented dialogue;
- invented motives;
- invented emotions;
- invented sensory details;
- unverified friendships or working relationships;
- employment inferred from credits;
- developer roles inferred from publisher names;
- legal continuity inferred from staff movement;
- photograph identifications inferred from appearance.

## Page Openings

Each narrative page should begin with a human or material anchor:

- a magazine listing;
- a line of code;
- a photograph with cautious provenance;
- a machine constraint;
- a named person where evidence supports the role;
- a place where evidence supports the connection.

The opening may be vivid in structure, but not fictional. Do not write what someone felt, heard, intended or said unless a source supports it.

## Evidence Labels

Use these labels consistently:

- `Confirmed`
- `Well supported`
- `First-person recollection`
- `Approximate`
- `Disputed`
- `Open question`
- `Provisional`

For public content levels use:

- `Flagship narrative story`
- `Curated profile`
- `Enriched archive record`
- `Index-only archive record`

For game pages use:

- `Tier 1: Full narrative game story`
- `Tier 2: Enriched profile`
- `Tier 3: Archive index record`

## People

Person pages may include:

- opening vignette;
- why the person matters;
- career narrative;
- roles and craft;
- verified games;
- verified company relationships;
- where they went next;
- approved quotations;
- photographs;
- evidence;
- open questions;
- related content.

Never infer employment from a game credit. Use phrases such as "credited on", "associated with", "published by", "worked for as a self-employed author" or "employment not established" according to the source.

## Studios

Studio pages may include:

- opening scene;
- place;
- origin;
- founders;
- business activity;
- people;
- games;
- platforms;
- technical strengths;
- magazine coverage;
- transitions;
- closure or acquisition;
- legal continuity;
- staff heritage;
- evidence;
- unresolved questions.

Separate legal continuity from staff heritage in both prose and diagrams.

## Games

Every game page must show its record level. Preserve platform-specific releases and credits.

Do not merge Spectrum, BBC, Electron, Commodore or other versions into one authorship record without evidence.

Do not calculate average review scores across publications or platform versions unless the methodology is explained and justified. This foundation does not calculate averages.

## Magazine Records

On relevant pages show:

- publication;
- issue;
- date;
- platform version;
- article type;
- reviewer or byline;
- structured score or award;
- concise editorial paraphrase;
- archive link.

Do not reproduce full articles, listings, scans or copyrighted images.

## Corrections

Corrections should say:

- what changed;
- why it changed;
- source or evidence used;
- date changed;
- whether the change affects a conclusion.

Do not silently upgrade contested claims.
