Tynesoft is the first place in this Phase 0 slice where software starts to look like local industry rather than only individual authorship.

The public evidence associates Tynesoft with Blaydon and a move from educational software into games, publishing, ports, licences and support for multiple incompatible 8-bit computers. The Centre for Computing History and Stairway to Hell records support catalogue breadth. Kevin Blake's retrospective portfolio supports production recollections, but those recollections remain testimony rather than official company records.

This page separates three things that are often blurred:

- Tynesoft as a software house and publisher.
- MicroValue as a related budget label requiring careful ownership evidence.
- Later staff or knowledge movement, which is not legal succession.

The most important current editorial rule is that Tynesoft must not become a shortcut for every later North East company. Staff heritage belongs in the story. Legal continuity needs legal evidence.
