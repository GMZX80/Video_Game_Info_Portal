In the early home-computer years, software could arrive as words on paper.

A magazine listing was not just a way to fill pages. It could be a distribution channel, a lesson, an installation ritual and a debugging exercise. The reader had to type the program in, notice mistakes, learn the machine's moods and sometimes change the result. The work of play and the work of making were closer together than they would later become.

The ZX Spectrum manual makes that closeness visible. BASIC was resident in ROM, so switching on the machine placed the owner near a prompt rather than a storefront. The manual explained commands such as `PEEK`, `POKE`, `USR`, `DATA`, `CLEAR` and `SAVE CODE`; the screen memory made technical constraints concrete. On the Spectrum, 6,912 bytes of display memory meant that game authors designed around 6,144 bitmap bytes and 768 colour-attribute bytes.

That is why printed listings matter to the North East story. They show a route from player to author before there were many local companies to join. Code moved through magazines, books, libraries, clubs, cassette swapping and friends. A teenager or hobbyist did not need a studio job before learning how game logic, graphics memory, loaders and machine-code calls fitted together.

This chapter is deliberately not a romance of bedroom genius. It is a practical story about circulation. The evidence supports the idea that listings helped people learn, distribute and modify programs. It does not tell us that every later professional learned in exactly the same way, nor does it prove a specific person's private chronology unless a source says so.

For Phase 0, the point is simpler and stronger: before the studios, there was a network. Paper was part of it.
